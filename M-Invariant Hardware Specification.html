3.2 Physical Structure

The SDT consists of:

  1. Substrate: p-type silicon, reference doping $N_A = 10^{16}$ cm$^{-3}$. The substrate provides the fixed reference density $ ho_0$.
  2. Channel: n-type inversion layer, doping profile tuned such that equilibrium carrier density $n_{\text{eq}} = n_0 \cdot \mathfrak{M}$. This requires golden-ratio doping: the channel doping concentration is $\mathfrak{M}$ times the substrate doping concentration.
  3. Gate: Metal-oxide stack with work function $\Phi_M$ adjusted to create a flat-band condition at $V_G = 0$ when the channel density equals $\mathfrak{M} \cdot n_0$. The gate oxide thickness $t_{ox}$ is chosen to satisfy:
    $$\frac{\epsilon_{ox}}{t_{ox}} = \mathfrak{M} \cdot \frac{\epsilon_{Si}}{W_{dep}}$$ Gate oxide capacitance matched to depletion capacitance via $\mathfrak{M}$-scaling.
  4. Source/Drain: n$^+$ regions with density $n^+ = 10^{20}$ cm$^{-3}$. These serve as scalar density reservoirs — they maintain fixed density regardless of channel state, analogous to voltage reservoirs in conventional CMOS.

3.3 The Density Differential Sensor

Reading $\mathfrak{D}$ requires a sensor that measures carrier density gradient, not voltage. The specification defines a capacitive density sensor:

$$\mathfrak{D}_{\text{measured}} = \frac{1}{C_{\text{sense}}} \cdot \frac{\partial Q_{\text{channel}}}{\partial \tau}$$ The sensor capacitance $C_{\text{sense}}$ converts charge variation to a measurable voltage that is proportional to the density differential.

The sensor is a split-gate structure: two gate capacitors on either side of the channel measure the charge imbalance. Because thermal noise affects both sides equally (scalar property), the differential measurement cancels it. This is the scalar common-mode rejection — the analog of differential signaling in conventional electronics, but operating on density rather than voltage.

At the chip boundary, a density-voltage translator converts between the internal scalar density domain and external voltage signals:

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{DD}} \cdot \frac{\mathfrak{D} - \mathfrak{D}_{\text{min}}}{\mathfrak{D}_{\text{max}} - \mathfrak{D}_{\text{min}}}$$ Linear mapping from density differential to voltage for external compatibility.

6.3 Power Delivery

The SDT requires density rails rather than voltage rails. In practice, these are implemented as:

  • $\rho_{\text{DD}}$ (density supply): Fixed carrier density $10^{20}$ cm$^{-3}$ (source/drain doping level)
  • $\rho_{\text{SS}}$ (density ground): Substrate reference density $10^{16}$ cm$^{-3}$

The power delivery network (PDN) is a density distribution network — heavily doped n$^+$ rails that maintain fixed carrier density across the die. This is electrically equivalent to a conventional VDD/VSS network but conceptually distinct: it supplies carriers, not potential.
